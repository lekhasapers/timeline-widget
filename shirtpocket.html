Early Challenges in Portability

Pre-1976

Before the development of the 5.25" disk drive, portable computers were virtually nonexistent. The lack of a standard size for removable storage posed a significant challenge to the emerging field of mobile computing.

Debate over "Shirt Pocket" Diskette Sizes

Early to Mid-1970s

As computer systems matured, industry experts and journalists debated which diskette size would be best suited for portable computing. Notably, computer manufacturer George Morrow suggested that the industry work with the clothing sector to design shirt pockets around a 5.25" format, highlighting the need for a truly portable storage medium.

Dysan’s 3.25" Diskette Initiative

Mid-1970s to Early 1980s

To address the market’s demand for portability without the extensive retooling required by 3" or 3.5" formats, Dysan introduced the 3.25" diskette. This design was a scaled-down version of the 5.25" diskette and was produced with minimal changes to existing manufacturing processes.

Software Publishing to Promote the 3.25" Format

Late 1970s to Early 1980s

In an effort to establish the 3.25" diskette as the standard for portable storage, Dysan initiated a software publishing business. By securing distribution deals with major software companies, Dysan ensured that key software titles were available on 3.25" media. Although this strategy briefly gave the 3.25" format some market traction, it ultimately failed to achieve widespread adoption.

Market Consolidation around the 3.5" Standard

Late 1970s to Early 1980s

Despite significant production of 3.25" diskettes and drives (e.g., the rare Seequa Chameleon 325), the market eventually consolidated around the 3.5" format. Major players such as Hewlett Packard, Apple, and IBM adopted the 3.5" diskette as the industry standard, demonstrating that factors like market inertia and strategic alliances often outweighed technical superiority.
